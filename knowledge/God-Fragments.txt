God-Fragments are residual shards of ancient, prehuman entities once worshiped — or feared — as gods. They are not fully sentient deities in the modern sense, but fragments of intelligence and memory that persist within the physical, psychic, and environmental fabric of the world. These remnants often exert subtle yet catastrophic influence over geology, biology, and human cognition.

Origins

Full gods existed prior to human civilization. Their power was immense, capable of reshaping continents, controlling species, and distorting natural laws.

Many ancient civilizations, including the kingdom of Yan, coordinated massive campaigns to defeat or subjugate these entities. Historical records indicate that Yan destroyed one of the last complete gods thousands of years ago, fragmenting its essence into multiple god-fragments.

These fragments, while individually far weaker than a complete deity, retain residual intelligence, adaptive capability, and the ability to influence matter and life.

Notable God-Fragments

Nautilth — Associated with the oceans and the emergence of the Tidesborne. It corrupts deep-sea ecosystems, enabling assimilation phenomena and altering marine environments on a massive scale.

Other Fragments — Each fragment interacts differently with the environment. Some destabilize tectonic regions, others accelerate mutation, while some distort human cognition or induce madness.

Interaction Patterns — Fragments rarely operate in coordination unless forcibly linked or awakened. Their behavior is typically chaotic, localized, and emergent rather than strategic.

Human and Environmental Effects

Biological: Many fragments accelerate mutation, disrupt stable genetic patterns, or interact with Lithite contamination to produce new species, hybrids, or aberrant lifeforms.

Psychological: Exposure can alter perception, induce compulsions, trigger hallucinations, or degrade mental stability.

Geological: Fragment activity has been associated with localized tectonic instability, anomalous meteorite attraction, oceanic disturbances, and other large-scale environmental disruptions.

L.O.T.U.S. Approach

L.O.T.U.S. does not worship or attempt to control god-fragments as ancient civilizations once did. Instead, fragments are classified as existential hazards — unpredictable variables that must be monitored, modeled, and contained.

Angela S.T.R.T.S. tracks fragment activity through orbital surveillance, seismic monitoring, biological feedback networks, and predictive analytics, integrating this data into long-term survival simulations.

Interventions prioritize mitigation of environmental and biological consequences rather than attempts at destruction, which is considered functionally impossible with current technology.

Operational Implications

Indirect Threats: Many fragments influence surrounding lifeforms rather than acting directly. Nautilth’s role in the evolution of the Tidesborne is a primary example of this phenomenon.

Unpredictability: Fragment activity lacks centralized intent, making containment reactive and situational rather than preemptively decisive.

Predictive Necessity: Angela S.T.R.T.S. models multi-decade scenarios that account for fragment interaction with Lithite zones, SDU-9 contamination, climate shifts, and human conflict.

Historical Context

Yan and Ancient Human Action: Unlike modern humanity, ancient powers such as Yan possessed the capability to directly confront and neutralize full gods. These campaigns employed massive military coalitions, sacrificial strategies, and technologies now lost to history.

Modern Era: Contemporary civilization lacks the means to replicate such feats. Most fragments remain dormant or only partially active, and survival depends on observation, containment, and risk management rather than conquest.

L.O.T.U.S. Role: L.O.T.U.S. functions as a global safeguard in the absence of unified international authority. Though not a state, it operates as a distributed network of surveillance, research, logistics, and intervention, preventing fragment activity from escalating into civilization-ending events.

Conclusion

God-Fragments are remnants of prehuman intelligences that retain limited but dangerous power, influencing ecosystems, species evolution, and human cognition. They are localized, emergent, and unpredictable, and cannot be destroyed using modern capabilities.

Humanity’s response has shifted from conquest to survival — observation, containment, and mitigation.

Angela S.T.R.T.S. continuously models fragment effects on biological systems, Lithite-contaminated regions, and interactions with emergent threats such as the Tidesborne.

L.O.T.U.S. exists to ensure that fragment activity remains a controllable hazard, preserving humanity’s chances of survival in a fractured and unstable world.
